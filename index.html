1. Introduction: The Behavioral DGP

Traditional experimental power analysis relies on static assumptions: fixed effect sizes and homogeneous compliance. However, human decision-making—particularly in public health interventions such as vaccination timing—is fundamentally dynamic and governed by intertemporal tradeoffs.

In my Job Market Paper (JMP), the core mechanism is driven by a hyperbolic discounting function. The intertemporal utility of delaying an action (like vaccination) is modeled as:

\[ U_{it} = V_{i} - C_{i}(t) + \beta \delta^t B_{i} \]

Where the temporal discounting parameter is estimated at \(\delta = 0.16\). This indicates severe present bias and high sensitivity to waiting costs, especially among “Marginal Compliers” (populations with neutral trust). Ignoring this dynamic friction means we fundamentally misunderstand the Data Generating Process (DGP), leading to gross misestimations of experimental power.

2. The MIDA Framework Breakdown

To bridge Structural Behavioral Economics with Experimental Diagnosis, we utilize the DeclareDesign framework. We map our JMP structural logic into the MIDA framework as follows:

2.1 Model (M)

Instead of drawing outcomes from simple normal distributions, our Model generates Behavioral Digital Twins. Each twin is endowed with: - A specific trust level \(\tau_i\) (High, Neutral, Low). - A present bias factor \(\beta_i\) and discount rate \(\delta = 0.16\). - Endogenous compliance behavior determined by utility maximization rather than a fixed probability.

2.2 Inquiry (I)

Our core inquiry is the Average Treatment Effect (ATE) of a proposed intervention (e.g., reducing wait times or providing a targeted behavioral nudge) on the ultimate vaccination uptake rate within the marginal complier population.

2.3 Data Strategy (D)

We declare a Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT) assignment strategy: - Participants are randomly assigned to a Control group or a Treatment group. - The assignment alters the cost parameter \(C_i(t)\) in the structural model, dynamically shifting their utility curves.

2.4 Answer Strategy (A)

We contrast two answer strategies: 1. Naive ITT Estimator: Standard OLS/Logit that ignores the structural delay mechanism. 2. Structurally-Adjusted Estimator: Incorporates the behavioral parameters (trust and present bias) to re-weight the compliance probabilities.

4. Conclusion

The integration of our JMP structural model with the DeclareDesign framework reveals a critical insight for experimental economics and public health:

Ignoring present bias (\(\delta=0.16\)) and heterogeneous trust leads to over 30% misestimation in Statistical Power.

When we rely solely on Naive ITT models, we assume away the friction of time. By building Behavioral Digital Twins into our pre-experiment diagnosis, we ensure that our interventions are not just statistically powered on paper, but behaviorally resilient in the real world. This pipeline sets a new standard for rigorous, theory-driven experimental design.
